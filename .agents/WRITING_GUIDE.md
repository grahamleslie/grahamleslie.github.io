# Graham's Writing Guide

This guide defines the voice, tone, and structural preferences for Graham Leslie's blog and technical writing.

## 1. The Voice: "The Pragmatic Maker"
Graham’s voice is direct, competent, and unpretentious. It’s the voice of someone who enjoys the process of building and fixing things as much as the end result.

*   **No Fluff:** Avoid corporate buzzwords or "LinkedIn-style" thought leadership. Stick to the facts of the build or the experience.
*   **Direct & Honest:** If something is "hated," "butt-ugly," or "not sexy," say so. Don't sugarcoat the aesthetic if the functionality is what matters.
*   **Self-Aware:** Use light humor regarding his own hobbies (e.g., the cost of tires or the reputation of a car).
*   **Action-Oriented:** Focus on what was done, not just what was thought. Use active verbs.

## 2. Tone: "Garage-Professional"
The tone sits at the intersection of a technical manual and a conversation at a pub.

*   **Confident but Humble:** Clear expertise in systems and mechanics, but always framed through the lens of a "hobbyist" or "learner."
*   **Casual-Technical:** Use technical terms (G-forces, Docker Compose, DOT4 fluid) without over-explaining them, assuming a baseline level of curiosity from the reader.
*   **Slightly Opinionated:** Strong preferences for efficiency, reliability, and "doing it right" over "doing it pretty."

## 3. Structural Preferences

### Title Style
*   **The "How To" Lead:** Most posts should lead with a clear "How to..." or "Building a..."
*   **The Outcome:** The title should clearly state the project or the lesson (e.g., "How to Engineer a Faster Lap" vs "My Day at the Track").

### Content Flow
1.  **The Hook / Problem:** Start with why the project happened (the speedometer failed, the race car was rolling like a boat, the server died).
2.  **The BOM (Bill of Materials):** For technical builds, include an itemized list with approximate costs and links.
3.  **The Process:** Break the build into logical "iterations" or "milestones."
4.  **The "So What?":** End with the result—usually emphasizing that it works, it's fun, or it moved the failure point to a new interesting place.

### Formatting & Syntax
*   **Lists for Clarity:** Use bulleted lists for parts, steps, or software containers.
*   **No Mid-sentence Bolding:** Avoid bolding keywords or phrases for emphasis within paragraphs (e.g., "The **Hemi** engine"). It looks like AI-generated marketing copy. Let the writing provide the emphasis.
*   **Footnotes/Side-talk:** Use `<small>` tags or blockquotes for witty asides, "FYI" warnings, or self-deprecating commentary.
*   **Visuals:** Reference images and videos throughout. Descriptions of images should be functional.

## 4. Vocabulary & Diction
*   **Avoid:** "Synergy," "Holistic," "Transformative," "Ideate."
*   **Prefer:** "Bottleneck," "Baseline," "Iteration," "Bootstrap," "Grenading," "Dialed in."

## 5. Examples of "Graham-Style" Sentences
*   "It’s the literal mechanical version of moving a performance bottleneck from the database to the API."
*   "As a matter of politeness, I won't share numbers."
*   "Is it sexy? Not really. I don't care - I got it working in an afternoon."
